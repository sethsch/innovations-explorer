About the project

This project was developed and submitted towards the capstone requirement for completion of the Masters of Science in Data Analysis and Visualization at the CUNY Graduate Center. The project was motivated by my professional experience with data mining and analytics development within a private philanthropic foundation, and inspired by recent research and scholarship in fields like computational social sciences, scientometrics and economic geography.

By making even a subset of government funded research more visible for a variety of analysts and a wider public, my hope is that the Public Innovations Explorer can foreground and provoke questions regarding the geography of knowledge production and how “innovation” is defined in relation to particular research agendas. Rather than advancing one particular analysis, the Explorer—along with its repository of scripts and processed data—can contribute to a variety of analyses while also highlighting the ways in which data modeling, analytics and visualization go hand in hand.

Central to this work is the peculiarity of the grant record as a type of data artifact. While both scientific publications and patent records are communicative artifacts whose primary purpose is directly supported by its existence as a physical or digital record, a grant record exists as an administrative artifact whose form as a data artifact is of secondary importance to its function as a financial exchange or transaction. In the landscape of research and knowledge production, grant records are an input that reflect attractive opportunities and priorities. Highlighting the what and where of research funding is one way to evaluate which research initiatives and agendas have been prioritized in the past, and imagine what research initiatives and agendas might be supported in the future.

Scientometrics

Use the explorer to retrieve district-level summaries regarding the research and technology transfer activities of small businesses, or to identify different awardees and get a sense of their research agendas. While you can always consult the central awards database at SBIR.gov to obtain more detail regarding individual grantees, the Public Innovations Explorer is intended to generate more locally-grounded portraits of specialized research activity. You might consider asking:

  • Where particular Federal agencies and departments tended to committ funding?
  • What are the characteristics of grantee companies in a particular district?
  • How are the research activities happening in a particular district tied to its specialized labor profile?
  • In what ways particular districts have or have not tended to specialize in certain areas of research?
  • How might research activities impact future economic activities in the district?

Use the parallel coordinates plot to filter the congressional districts on the map, and identify hot spots and cold spots for different kinds of underlying economic activities. Click on a congressional district to see a summary of its funded research activities, labor profile and topics extracted from the funded research taking place there.
